Get Started

Starting Your Hiking Journey

Arizona is a wonderful state for a vast variety of outdoor activities. Whether you are an outdoor enthusiast or not, there are outdoor adventures for everyone in Arizona. Hiking is no different, as it is of the best ways to enjoy nature in Arizona, get exercise, and explore the unique geography of the state. If you are new to hiking, the most important thing is to start simple and easy and improve your skills and endurance over time.

You generally should begin by first tackling the shorter, flatter, and easier trails rather than the most strenuous and advanced ones. This will help you learn to stay steady walking on uneven ground, how to best manage your energy, know what supplies you need for yourself, and know your physical limits.

Essentials To Always Bring

Make sure to buy boots make specifically for hiking that fit you well and are comfortable for you. You will have be relying on them for miles of potentially rough terrain, so good footwear is crucial. In Arizona's hot and dry climate with unrelenting year long sunshine, proper clothing and sun protection are also critical. Make sure you have light colored, loose fitting, cool and breathable clothing, as well as a wide brimmed hat and plenty of sunscreen. In Arizona, having enough water on you is one of the most important things to consider. The general rule of thumb is to bring at least 1 liter of water per person for every 2 hours of hiking, and more if it is a hot day or if the trail is a strenuous one.

Make sure to bring snacks, ideally ones that are high in sodium/sugar and carbohydrates, as you will be needing the energy and electrolytes to keep yourself going. Use a GPS app on your phone or a physical map to make sure you stay on trail and do not get lost. Use trekking poles for trails that have rougher terrain or steep elevation gain. Make sure to have a comfortable backpack to carry all your supplies, and consider a camelbak for carrying your water. Other things recommended to bring are sunglasses, a portable first aid kit, a whistle, and a flashlight or headlamp. Consider to have a way to signal or indicate for help in case of an emergency in locations with low to no cell phone reception.

General Safety Guidelines

Before heading out, check the weather and make sure the trail is suitable for your skill level. Let someone know where you are going, especially if you plan to hike alone. Begin hikes early in the day or later in the evening to avoid the hottest hours and intense sunshine. If possible, try to hike in the cooler months for the lower elevation desert trails.

Make sure that your phone is fully charged before leaving for a hike and consider bringing a portable phone charger. Stay on marked, clearly visible trails whenever possible. Watch your footing, take breaks when needed, and do not push yourself too hard. Make sure to turn back early if you start feeling exhausted or if you approach half of your water supply.

Building Your Hiking Experience

The more you hike, the more confident you will become. Try to start with the easier, more local trails, learn your limits, and gradually try longer or more challenging hikes.

Hiking should be enjoyable, not overwhelming. Do not be discouraged if some trails feel too difficult for you at first. With preparation and practice, you can safely experience many of Arizona's beautiful trails and outdoor regions, and become an experienced hiker who can conquer the more strenuous and advanced trails within Arizona.
